About Me

My research focuses on electron transfer (redox) processes that affect contemporary environmental problems such as climate change and environmental pollution. The redox biogeochemistry of carbon (C) and iron (Fe) are of special interest to me as these two elemental cycles control the release of greenhouse gases and the fate of organic and inorganic contaminants. Housed in the larger Geomicrobiology group at the Eberhard Karls University of Tübingen, my group leverages molecular, bench scale, and field techniques to probe processes at the mechanistic level and connect them to large scale phenomena of global relevance.

Current Projects

Greenhouse gas release from coastal wetlands (referred to as “blue carbon”)

In an exciting new project funded by the Ministry of Science, Research and Arts of Baden Württemberg, we are looking at carbon cycling in coastal wetlands along the north coast of Germany. Specifically, we are looking at how iron and sulfur control the release of the greenhouse gases CO2 and CH4 under present climate conditions and how that may change due to elevated temperatures and sea level rise.

Iron-carbon cycling in permafrost peatlands

We are investigating how the cycles of iron and carbon are coupled along permafrost thaw gradients and what this coupling means for the release of greenhouse gases. Our fieldwork is based in Abisko, Sweden.

Size-dependent interactions between iron minerals and trace elements

In a project funded by the Chinese Scholarship Council, we investigate how iron (oxyhydr)oxides along the size continuum interact with trace elements under reducing and oxidizing conditions.

Fundamental processes at the iron-particulate organic matter interface

We investigate sorption and redox processes at the interface of iron (Fe(II) and Fe(III)) and particulate organic matter (POM) at the bulk and molecular scale.

Further projects

I’m also involved in other projects looking at iron-nitrogen-arsenic cycling in fertilized paddy soils and the role of iron metabolizing microorganisms in Banded Iron Formations (BIFs).
